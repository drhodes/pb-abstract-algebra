Proposition 1.2.11. For any sets `A, B, C \subseteq U`, the following properties hold:

  1. Idempotency: \[ A \cup A = A\quad \text{and}\quad A \cap A = A \]
  2. Identity: \[ A \cup \varnothing = A\quad \text{and}\quad A \cap U = A.\]
  3. Absorption: \[ A \cup U = U,\quad A \cap \varnothing = \varnothing \quad \text{and}\quad A \setminus \varnothing = A.\]
  4. Complements: \[ A \cup A' = U,\quad A \cap A' = \varnothing,\quad \text{and} \quad A \setminus A = \varnothing. \]
  5. Commutativity: \[ A \cup B = B \cup A \quad \text{and} \quad A \cap B = B \cap A. \]
  6. Associativity: \[ A \cup (B \cup C) = (A \cup B) \cup C \quad \text{and} \quad A \cap (B \cap C) = (A \cap B) \cap C. \]
  7. Distributivity: \[ A \cup (B \cap C) = (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)\quad \text{and} \quad A \cap (B \cup C) = (A \cap B) \cup (A \cap C). \]
  8. De Morgan’s Laws: \[ (A \cap B)' = A' \cup B' and (A \cup B)' = A' \cap B'. \]

Proof. Many of these identities are straightforward to prove and several will be verified in the homework. To illustrate the basic method for proving two sets are equal, we prove the first distributive law:

`A \cup (B \cap C) = (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)`.

To prove that two sets are equal, we show that each is a subset of the other.

First, we show that `A \cup (B \cap C) \subseteq (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)`. Let `x \in A \cup (B \cap C)`. Then `x \in A` or `x \in (B \cap C)`. If `x \in A`, since `A \subseteq A \cup B` and `A \subseteq A \cup C`, it follows that `x \in (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)`. If instead `x \in (B \cap C)`, then `x \in B` and `x \in C`. Since `B \subseteq A \cup B` and `C \subseteq A \cup C`, again `x \in (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)`. Thus, `A \cup (B \cap C) \subseteq (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)`.

Next, we show that `(A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C) \subseteq A \cup (B \cap C)`. Let `x \in (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)`. Then `x \in A \cup B` and `x \in A \cup C`. If `x \in A`, then clearly `x \in A \cup (B \cap C)`. Otherwise, if `x \notin A`, then `x` must be in both `B` and `C`, so `x \in B \cap C`, which implies `x \in A \cup (B \cap C)`. Therefore, `(A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C) \subseteq A \cup (B \cap C)`.

Combining these two inclusions, we conclude that `A \cup (B \cap C) = (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)`.
