Theorem 1.2.21

Let `f: A \to B`, `g: B \to C`, and `h: C \to D`. Then:

  • (a) The composition of mappings is associative, that is, \[(h \circ g) \circ f = h \circ (g \circ f).\]
  • (b) If `f` and `g` are both surjective, then `g \circ f` is surjective;
  • (c) If `f` and `g` are both injective, then `g \circ f` is injective;
  • (d) If `f` and `g` are both bijective, then `g \circ f` is bijective.

Proof

Let `a \in A`. Then

\[[(h \circ g) \circ f](a) = (h \circ g)(f(a)) = h(g(f(a))) = h((g \circ f)(a)) = [h \circ (g \circ f)](a),\]

which proves (a).

Let `c \in C`. Since `g` is surjective, there exists some `b \in B` such that `g(b) = c`. Likewise, since `f` is surjective, there exists some `a \in A` such that `f(a) = b`. Thus,

\[(g \circ f)(a) = g(f(a)) = g(b) = c,\]

which proves (b). Part (c) is left as an exercise to the student, and part (d) follows immediately from (b) and (c).
