Introduction to Group Theory (0:01)

This lecture introduces mathematical group theory at a level suitable for enthusiastic undergraduates and first-year graduate students. The course focuses on examples rather than proofs and covers topics such as finite groups, symmetric groups, free groups, reflection groups, and more. Each video will link to the next lecture and the complete lecture playlist.

What Is a Group? (0:53)

A group is defined as the collection of all symmetries of an object. A symmetry is a mapping from the object to itself that preserves its structure.

Examples of Groups via Symmetries (1:09)

Platonic Solids (1:13)

Dodecahedron (1:13)

The dodecahedron can be rotated about several axes:

  • Rotations by `1/5` of a revolution (order `5`).
  • Rotations by `1/3` of a revolution (order `3`).
  • Flips (order `2` symmetries).

Counting the rotations: choosing one of 12 faces as the base and then 5 possible rotations per face gives `12 \times 5 = 60` rotational symmetries. If reflections are included, the total symmetry count becomes `120`.

Other Solids (1:42)

  • Cube and Octahedron: Each has `24` symmetries.
  • Icosahedron: `60` symmetries.
  • Tetrahedron: `12` symmetries.

Some solids share the same number of symmetries, indicating that their symmetry groups are isomorphic.

Cyclic Groups (Rotations of a Star) (3:52)

A star with five identical points has a cyclic symmetry: rotating it by multiples of 1/5 of a revolution produces a cyclic group of order `5`. In general, cyclic groups exist for any given order.

The Group of Integers (`\mathbb{Z}`) (5:00)

Consider a line marked with regularly spaced points. Shifting all points by a fixed number corresponds to a symmetry. These shifts form a group isomorphic to the integers under addition.

Rubik’s Cube (6:03)

The Rubik’s Cube is a famous example of a group with a very large number of symmetries. Its various twists and turns demonstrate complex group behavior, though the focus here is on understanding the idea of symmetry.

Symmetric Groups (6:46)

For a set of six identical objects, any permutation is a symmetry. The number of symmetries is `6! = 720`, and this group is denoted as `S_6`, the symmetric group on six elements.

Linear Groups (Symmetries of Vector Spaces) (8:00)

In a vector space (for example, `\mathbb{R}^3`), symmetries are given by linear maps that preserve vector addition and scalar multiplication. Only the invertible maps (matrices with nonzero determinant) qualify, forming the General Linear Group, denoted `GL_3(\mathbb{R})`.

Galois Theory and Complex Numbers (9:48)

Considering the complex numbers `\mathbb{C}`, an important symmetry is complex conjugation (mapping `z = x + iy` to `x - iy`). This symmetry, which preserves addition and multiplication, forms a group of order `2` and is a basic example in Galois theory.

Groups in Physics (11:11)

In physics, groups describe space-time symmetries:

  • Lorentz Group: Symmetries of space-time when a point is fixed.
  • Poincaré Group: Symmetries when no point is fixed.

Other important groups include `SU(3)`, which is relevant in quantum chromodynamics (the study of quarks).

Groups of Groups (12:32)

Groups can themselves have symmetries. For example, the cyclic group `\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z}` (with elements `0, 1, 2, 3, 4` under addition modulo `5`) has nontrivial symmetries that preserve its group structure.

Axioms for a Group (14:13)

A group is a set equipped with a binary operation (often called multiplication) satisfying three key axioms:

  1. Associativity: For all elements `a`, `b`, and `c`, ` (a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c) `.
  2. Identity Element: There exists an element `e` (which may be denoted as `1`, `0`, or `E`) such that for every element `a`, `e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a`.
  3. Inverses: Every element `a` has an inverse `a^{-1}` such that `a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e`.

Goals and Themes in Group Theory (17:13)

The main goals in group theory include:

  • Classification: Classifying all “interesting” groups up to isomorphism. Two groups are isomorphic if there exists a bijective map between them that preserves the group operation, meaning they are essentially the same despite relabeling.
  • Representation Theory: Studying the ways in which groups act on sets (permutation representations) or on vector spaces (linear representations). This provides insights into the structure of groups by relating them to well-understood groups like symmetric and general linear groups.

Completeness of Group Axioms (20:00)

The three axioms (associativity, identity, inverses) form a complete set for defining a group. That is, any set that satisfies these axioms is isomorphic to the symmetry group of some mathematical object. This result is known as Cayley’s Theorem and will be explored further in subsequent lectures.
