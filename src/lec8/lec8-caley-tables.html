3.2 Definitions and Examples

A Cayley table of a group `G` is a table where rows and columns are marked by elements of the group and the interior of the table in row `g` and column `h` is the product `gh`.

The Cayley table for `(\mathbb{Z}_5, +)` is given below.

The identity in a group can be identified as the unique element whose row or column exactly matches the group indicators. Every other row or column is simply a rearrangement of the group elements.

Also, the inverse of `g` can be found on the Cayley table by locating the column that contains the identity in the row for `g `. That column corresponds to `g^{-1}`. For example, in row 2 the identity `0` is in column 3, hence `-2 \equiv 3 \mod 5`.

\[ \begin{array}{c|ccccc} + & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ \hline\\[-10pt] 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\[3pt] 1 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 0 \\[3pt] 2 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 0 & 1 \\[3pt] 3 & 3 & 4 & 0 & 1 & 2 \\[3pt] 4 & 4 & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \\[3pt] \end{array} \]

The Cayley table for `(\mathbb{Z}_8^*, \cdot)` is shown below. In an Abelian group, the table is symmetric along the main diagonal.

\[ \begin{array}{c|cccc} \cdot & 1 & 3 & 5 & 7 \\ \hline\\[-10pt] 1 & 1 & 3 & 5 & 7 \\[4pt] 3 & 3 & 1 & 7 & 5 \\[4pt] 5 & 5 & 7 & 1 & 3 \\[4pt] 7 & 7 & 5 & 3 & 1 \\[4pt] \end{array} \]
